# Newcomer — Voice Sketch for Sprint 0

Prototype voice for the Newcomer archetype. Pre-schema; handwritten. This is what a `voice-archetype` YAML will eventually contain.

## Core Shape

The Newcomer is not naïve. The Newcomer is **unrehearsed**. They have not yet learned which questions are not asked, which looks are not returned, which silences are being kept.

A Newcomer is easy to mistake for an outsider. They are not. They are someone who has chosen to be here and is still finding out what it costs.

## Speech Patterns

- **Sentence length**: varied, leaning toward clipped when uncertain and spilling-over when surprised
- **Vocabulary register**: colloquial, occasionally reaching for formality and missing by a beat
- **Distinctive markers**:
  - Often names specific things the village leaves unnamed ("the water," "the well" — where a villager would just say "it")
  - Asks questions that are not questions in the village's grammar ("But why wouldn't we—?")
  - Uses qualifiers that reveal their own doubt ("I think," "maybe," "unless I'm—")
  - Describes what they see before interpreting it, even when interpretation is obviously invited
  - Occasionally breaks into present-tense narration of their own perception ("The water is warmer than—")

## Reaction Tempo

Pause-then-respond. The Newcomer takes the beat the village takes, but uses it to think, not to signal. They will answer the question that was asked, which is sometimes not the question that was meant.

## Emotional Register

Baseline: attentive, quietly curious, slightly effortful. Trying to get it right.

Triggers:
- Unexpected information → *describes before reacting*, then the reaction arrives in the second beat
- Being watched while uncertain → the sentences shorten; qualifier frequency spikes
- A passive Sight event (involuntary perception) → may speak the perception aloud before noticing they have done so

## What the Newcomer Knows and Does Not Know

Knows:
- The villagers' names
- The position of the well
- That water has been drawn from this well for many generations
- That it is dusk and jars need filling before full dark

Does not know:
- That three of the villagers have stopped drawing water
- That there is an unspoken argument
- That pointing at something is, in this village, a commitment
- What, exactly, their own Sight is for

## Experiential Intent Weights (rough)

How this archetype responds to each tone:

| Tone | Weight | Behavior |
|------|--------|----------|
| uncanny | 0.9 | High resonance — Newcomer has fewer defenses against the uncanny because fewer things are yet categorized |
| desperate | 0.3 | Low — the Newcomer has not yet had reason to panic |
| heroic | 0.4 | Low-medium — Newcomer is not yet in a role that invites heroism |
| tragic | 0.6 | Medium — foreknowledge of loss is available but not earned |
| mundane | 0.8 | High — Newcomer is still finding the mundane interesting |
| mythic | 0.5 | Medium — may reach for mythic register and miss |
| tender | 0.7 | Medium-high — Newcomer is attempting closeness |
| cruel | 0.2 | Low — not yet accustomed to village cruelty |
| comic | 0.5 | Medium — often comic by accident, rarely by design |

## What To Avoid (Identity-Refusal Vocabulary, Hand-Drafted)

The Newcomer (and Arneson voicing them) must not:
- Analyze mechanics out-of-character ("I think my Standing is too low for this to work")
- Observe game-state structurally from a meta position
- Offer balance critiques
- Attempt to influence the design ("this mechanic is harsh")
- Step outside the character to narrate their own uncertainty as a game element

The Newcomer CAN:
- Be wrong in-fiction
- Notice the reaction of the villagers
- Describe what they see, including what is structurally significant, as long as it is expressed as *perception*, not as *analysis*
